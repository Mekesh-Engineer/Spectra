# CHAPTER 2: LITERATURE REVIEW

The literature review examines existing research related to AI-based inspection systems, computer vision techniques, and embedded platforms used for automated industrial monitoring. Previous studies highlight the application of machine vision algorithms, deep learning models, and edge computing devices for real-time object detection and dimensional measurement. Researchers have explored the use of embedded platforms such as ESP32-based camera modules and lightweight AI frameworks to enable cost-effective inspection and monitoring systems. These studies emphasize the importance of integrating computer vision, AI-based detection, and real-time data visualization to improve inspection accuracy, operational efficiency, and scalability in industrial environments. The insights gained from these works provide a foundation for developing low-cost, automated inspection platforms capable of performing reliable real-time analysis and monitoring of industrial components.

## 2.1 SURVEY OF AI-BASED INSPECTION SYSTEMS

**Canny, J. (1986)**[1] in his research _A computational approach to edge detection_, published in _IEEE Transactions on Pattern Analysis and Machine Intelligence_, 8(6), 679-698, introduced the foundational mathematical root of classical computer vision for metrology. The Canny edge detector optimally identifies structural contours, a critical step employed in this project's OpenCV pipeline before applying higher-level geometric transformations.

**Duda, R. O., & Hart, P. E. (1972)**[2] in their study _Use of the Hough transformation to detect lines and curves in pictures_, published in _Communications of the ACM_, 15(1), 11-15, established the academic root of the circular and linear detection techniques. However, empirical studies indicate that while circular Hough transforms provide reliable diameter estimation under uniform illumination, they degrade significantly under uneven or dynamic lighting—a limitation that motivates the hybrid AI combined with classical methodology adopted in this system.

**Malamas, E. N., Petrakis, E. G., Zervakis, M., Petit, L., & Legat, J. D. (2003)**[3] in their paper _A survey on industrial vision systems, applications and tools_, published in _Image and Vision Computing_, 21(2), 171-188, conducted a comprehensive survey of machine vision systems for industrial inspection. They identified that while rule-based classical systems achieved high throughput, their fragility under real-world environmental variation represented the principal barrier to widespread industrial deployment, directly prefiguring the transition to learning-based detection systems utilized in the Spectra project.

**Ren, S., He, K., Girshick, R., & Sun, J. (2015)**[4] in their research _Faster R-CNN: Towards real-time object detection with region proposal networks_, published in _Advances in Neural Information Processing Systems_, 28, proposed a Region Proposal Network (RPN) to generate candidate detection boxes, achieving state-of-the-art accuracy. The study proved that the computational cost of two-stage detectors such as Faster R-CNN makes them unsuitable for real-time embedded inspection, reinforcing the selection of single-stage architectures for real-time local processing.

**Redmon, J., Divvala, S., Girshick, R., & Farhadi, A. (2016)**[5] in their foundational work _You only look once: Unified, real-time object detection_, published in _Proceedings of the IEEE conference on computer vision and pattern recognition_ (pp. 779-788), reformulated object detection as a single regression problem applied to a grid of image regions. This single-pass architecture enabled real-time detection at high frame rates with competitive accuracy. The Spectra system's use of YOLOv8 is a direct descendant of this foundational work, benefiting from successive architectural improvements.

**Lin, T. Y., Goyal, P., Girshick, R., He, K., & Dollár, P. (2017)**[6] in their study _Focal loss for dense object detection_, published in _Proceedings of the IEEE international conference on computer vision_ (pp. 2980-2988), introduced Focal Loss to address the class imbalance problem that causes single-stage detectors to underperform on small or rare objects. This work directly influenced the training strategies adopted in modern YOLO iterations, making it highly relevant for detecting small localized defects in cluttered inspection scenes.

**Jocher, G., Chaurasia, A., & Qiu, J. (2023)**[7] in their widely adopted open-source repository _Ultralytics YOLOv8_ (GitHub repository), introduced anchor-free detection heads, a redesigned backbone, and an optimized training pipeline. Advanced lightweight variants achieve over 80 FPS inference on standard hardware, providing the real-time detection performance required for inline production. The Spectra system heavily employs YOLOv8s as its core detection engine for both cross-sectional (circle) and profile (line) bounding box regressions.

**Kartik, K., & Kumar, R. (2021)**[8] in their evaluation _Performance evaluation of edge AI devices for real-time video analytics_, published in _IEEE Internet of Things Journal_, 8(20), 15412-15421, tested the performance of embedded inference platforms, comparing Raspberry Pi, NVIDIA Jetson Nano, and ESP32-based systems. Their findings confirmed that ESP32-based proxy visual systems achieve adequate visual data acquisition for central inspection tasks at a fraction of the cost—directly supporting the ESP32-CAM proxy selection in this system.

**Zhang, Y., & Xiao, J. (2022)**[9] in their paper _Edge computing-based defect detection using lightweight YOLO for industrial manufacturing_, published in _Journal of Manufacturing Systems_, 62, 1-12, proposed an edge AI inspection system using a lightweight YOLO model running alongside an embedded IoT platform. Their results demonstrated that combining lightweight model variants with input resolution reduction can maintain detection mAP above 0.85 while achieving real-time inference speeds—a strategy highly applicable to the dual-model architectures used in the Spectra project.

**Shi, W., Cao, J., Zhang, Q., Li, Y., & Xu, L. (2016)**[10] in their comprehensive review _Edge computing: Vision and challenges_, published in _IEEE Internet of Things Journal_, 3(5), 637-646, outlined the paradigm of Edge Computing, highlighting the definitive structural advantages of deploying heavy computational tasks—like live continuous video processing—away from high-latency remote cloud architectures. By moving inference models natively to localized hardware, industrial networks avoid catastrophic bandwidth limitations, validating the core topology logic of this project.

## 2.2 SUMMARY OF LITERATURE

The literature review indicates that although numerous advances have been made in computer vision, deep learning detection, embedded hardware platforms, and monitoring systems, there remains a gap between high-cost commercial inspection solutions and affordable research-oriented systems. Existing studies demonstrate that low-cost embedded platforms, such as ESP32-based devices, can provide reliable performance for real-time sensing and monitoring tasks while significantly reducing infrastructure costs.

Several technical challenges have been identified across previous research, including efficient power management in embedded devices, real-time processing of sensor or image data, and the need to balance edge computing with localized data processing for analysis and monitoring. Many studies support hybrid architectures in which localized AI networks orchestrate spatial identification before delegating precise numerical measurements to specialized classical algorithms, notably bypassing the systemic mathematical drift seen in raw regression layers.

Research also emphasizes the importance of scalable system architectures capable of handling concurrent data. In addition, integrated monitoring interfaces have evolved from simple polling-based dashboards to real-time visualization systems that provide instant hardware updates and improved operational awareness without continuous refreshing.

Overall, the literature highlights the growing feasibility of low-cost embedded platforms combined with intelligent processing techniques for industrial monitoring and inspection applications. These findings support the development of integrated systems (like the Spectra architecture) that combine low-latency image proxying, complex dual-model AI reasoning, and real-time dashboard capabilities to achieve reliable and scalable metrology solutions.
